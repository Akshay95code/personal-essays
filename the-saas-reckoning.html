The SaaS Reckoning

Essay — May 2026

It has already started. A solo founder in Austin built a functional CRM for her consulting practice in a weekend — no engineers, no budget, no Salesforce subscription. A high school teacher in Manchester created a custom gradebook that does exactly what he needs and nothing he doesn't. A small logistics company replaced a five-seat Monday.com plan with something their operations manager described in plain English to an AI agent on a Tuesday afternoon. These are not edge cases anymore. They are the opening act.

The SaaS industry was built on a simple insight: most people can't write software, so sell them software that's good enough. The trade-off was always implicit — you would get 80% of what you needed, pay monthly for the privilege, and live with the other 20% forever. That bargain made companies like Salesforce, HubSpot, ServiceNow, and Zendesk worth hundreds of billions of dollars. Vibe coding breaks the bargain. When describing what you want is all it takes to build it, "good enough" stops being good enough.

The companies most exposed are the ones selling horizontal tools — software that tries to be useful to everyone and ends up being perfect for no one. Project management platforms. CRMs. Customer support ticketing systems. HR workflow tools. These products are essentially interfaces on top of databases, wrapped in enough features to justify a contract. An AI agent can produce the same thing in hours, tuned precisely to how a specific team actually works. The moat these companies thought they had — integrations, data lock-in, enterprise sales relationships — will erode slower than the product advantage, but it will erode.

In five to ten years, I expect the SaaS graveyard to include names that currently feel untouchable. Not because they built bad products, but because the category of "generic software for generic workflows" will shrink dramatically. What survives will be the genuinely hard infrastructure — the payment rails, the cloud platforms, the security and compliance layers — things where depth of engineering still matters and where the consequences of getting it wrong are severe. Nobody is going to vibe code their way to a SOC 2 audit or a distributed database. But a sales pipeline? A support queue? A project tracker? Those are already gone in spirit. The contracts just haven't expired yet.

I don't think this is a tragedy. The SaaS era produced real value — it brought software to organizations that could never have afforded bespoke development. Vibe coding is just the next step in the same direction: software that fits even better, for even more people, at even lower cost. The companies that figure out how to ride this wave — building for builders, offering the layers that AI can't yet replace — will do fine. The ones that keep selling the same horizontal product at the same price as if nothing has changed are going to have a very difficult board meeting sometime around 2029.

Written by Akshay Sharma — Applied AI Product Manager
