The Empty Whiteboard

January 2026

For most of the software era, the bottleneck was building. Getting from idea to working product meant months of development, a budget, a team, and a willingness to bet a large chunk of time on something that might not work. Most ideas never made it past the whiteboard, not because they were bad, but because the cost of finding out whether they were good was too high.

That bottleneck is collapsing. You can describe what you want in plain English and have something working in an afternoon. The cost of a first version has dropped so far, so fast, that the conversation has shifted entirely. The question is no longer whether you can build this. It is whether you should. And that question turns out to be much harder.

When execution is cheap, the empty whiteboard gets more intimidating, not less. Before, you could blame the technical barrier. The idea was good; you just needed a developer and a runway. Now there is no excuse. If you have not started, it is because you have not decided. And deciding what to build, from the full space of everything that could be built, is a kind of vertigo most people are not prepared for.

This is where prioritization, taste, and judgment become the rarest things in the room. Anyone can fill a whiteboard with ideas in twenty minutes. Fewer people can stare at an empty one, resist the pull of what is easy or familiar, and write down the right question. That pause before you commit, the moment where you ask what actually needs to exist, is where the real work happens. It is slow, uncomfortable work. No model does it for you.

The builders who will matter most over the next decade are not the ones who learn to use every new tool as it ships. They are the ones who develop the discipline to sit with an empty whiteboard. To hold the discomfort of not knowing without rushing to fill it. To ask what is worth building before asking how. That has always been the harder skill. It just took cheap execution to make that obvious.

Written by Akshay Sharma, Applied AI Product Manager
